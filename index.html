Wastewater-Based Epidemiology (WBE)

Wastewater-Based Epidemiology (WBE) at IIT Madras

We are establishing a new research center to test municipal wastewater for pathogens and chemicals and make the data available to the public and other potential stakeholders. The facility will act as an intelligence unit to track and prevent disease outbreaks in its early stages. The data will be analyzed and made available to the public through online real-time dashboard. We aim to extend this facility to other parts of India and develop a national health monitoring dashboard.

The WBE study will be performed by analyzing biological and chemical human urinary biomarkers in wastewater. The WBE tool will be used to track pollutants, pesticides, licit and illicit drugs, and personal care products to which the population is exposed.

We recently created an online dashboard to track SARS-CoV-2 viruses circulating in IIT Madras communities.

View Dashboard

We will also work on building a hydro-informatics platform for cities with appropriate modelling tools to give spatiotemporal information on the data derived from WBE studies.

The WBE will also be employed to detect and track antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes due to excessive consumption of antibiotics, emerging zoonotic diseases, and other infectious diseases.

Our data will provide an early warning to policymakers, health practitioners, public health officials, and the public of potential public health threats and help them in preparedness. Apart from SARS CoV-2, several other pathogens prevailing in a population will be detected through wastewater analysis. We are actively planning to set up a genome sequencing facility to identify variants of concern circulating in communities.

Typical WBE processes include wastewater sample collection and processing followed by analysis on polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and mass spectrometry (MS), combined with data analytics. The new center at IIT Madras will tackle the need to understand public health better and effectively predict and monitor future COVID-19-like pandemics.
